General Butchery Guidelines

  1. Proteins are prepared by the butcher by seaming one muscle from another muscle, creating a usable product for the chef.
  2. All muscles are separated from other muscles by a line of fat or an obvious bone.
  3. When seaming out muscles, use the tip of your knife and pull with your hands. Let gravity help you.
  4. Knife Work First - Anatomy Second: when you learn to seam one muscle from another, you can then proceed to learn more about anatomy.
  5. Always keep your work surface organized dry and clean
  6. Protect your non-knife hand
  7. Don't pick up anything up unless you know where you will put it down
  8. Knives are used to cut meat, saws are used to cut bone, not the other way around
  9. The more a muscle is used the more tough it will become, also it will be more flavourful. The more tender cuts tend to be more expensive and less flavourful.
  10. Try to leave as little meat on the bone as possible. (Unless the point is to have a bone with lots of meat, as with ribs or rib steaks)

Knife rules-

  1. Point the tip down when walking- announce loudly “Knife” so others know that there is a potential danger. Never ever walk with a knife pointed in front of you.
  2. When passing a knife to someone, present the handle to them, never hand them the blade.
  3. Keep your knife sharp: dull knives are dangerous as you have to apply more pressure and if you slip you can get a bad cut.
  4. Keep your knives dry, your hand can slip over a handle onto the blade and your fingers will get sliced.
  5. Never leave blades in a sink so that you or someone else can get cut.
  6. Let a falling knife fall, do not try to catch it.
  7. Keep your knives clean

Cutting rules: It takes practice to become proficient as a butcher or meat cutter, you will make mistakes, but you will get better. In a butcher shop it takes time to butcher animals into fabricated cuts. Time costs money when you take into account wages. A general rule when cutting and determining how much meat to leave on bone or skin and how much time to dedicate to it is to follow the 5% rule.
5% Rule: If there is more than 5% of meat on a piece of connective tissue, bone or fat, take the time to remove it to make usable trim. If there is less than 5% meat leave it where it is.
Usable trim: edible meat that is trimmed off of a fabricated cut or bone or fat. Used to make other value added products like sausage or burgers or meat pies.
