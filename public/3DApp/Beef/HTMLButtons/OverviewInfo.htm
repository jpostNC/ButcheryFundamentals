Beef Rib: Primal cut, fabricated cuts are ribeye, prime rib. Very tender, second most expensive cut of beef.

Beef Short loin: a primal cut, contains part of the tenderloin, and the striploin. Fabricated cuts are the porterhouse and T-Bone.

Beef Sirloin (of Beef): a Primal cut , comes from the French term surlonge which means under the loin.

Bottom sirloin: part of the sirloin in the abdominal region

Carcass: In butchery carcass refers to the body of an animal after it has been slaughtered and eviscerated.

Collagen: Collagen fibers hold bundles of meat fibers together. Younger animal's collagen have fewer crosslinks and this make them easier to cook. Tender cuts have a relatively thinner mesh of collagens thus allowing them to be tender while tougher cuts will have a more numerous collagen fiber therefore making them more resistant to heat.

Connective tissue: Connects the skeletal muscles to the bones, it also gives structure to other tissues or organs in the body.

Locomotive muscles: These are muscles in the neck, fore and hind limbs that are used to propel an animal forward. Since they are used frequently there is a lot of connective tissue. The more the muscle moves, the more connective tissue exists, that means tougher muscles and more flavour. Good for low slow cooking like braising and also for grinding.

Shoulder: Clod region, upper part of shoulder/ arm of beef

Support muscles: The muscles that encompass the support are the rib, loin, sirloin. These muscles like their name suggest are supportive of the locomotive muscles. They tend to be tender as they are not used for mobility.
